Standard Process Side Effects: What to Know

A plain-language overview of reported reactions, contraindications, and who should be cautious with Standard Process whole-food supplements (brand-wide review hub).

Most people on Standard Process don't notice much. The ones who do react usually run into one of these:

Most Commonly Reported Reactions

Across user reports and practitioner observation, the side effects most often associated with Standard Process fall into a few categories:

  • Doing the 21-Day Purification Program and feeling rough for the first 3-5 days — headache, tired, loose stools. That's mostly the calorie cut talking, not the supplements themselves
  • Feeling wired or having trouble sleeping on the glandular line — Drenamin, Symplex F, things like that — especially if you're also drinking caffeine or on thyroid medication
  • Having Hashimoto's or Graves' and being put on Thytrophin PMG without anyone asking. Talk to your doctor first
  • Bright-yellow urine on Cataplex B or G. Totally harmless. Just the riboflavin
  • Feeling slightly off in your stomach for the first week on any of the herbal MediHerb stuff or the concentrated-vegetable formulas. Usually passes
  • Standard herb allergies on the MediHerb line if you're sensitive to ragweed-family plants

Who Should Be Cautious

Don't start without your doctor knowing if you have autoimmune thyroid disease and you're being put on the PMG line. Skip the glandulars and PMG entirely if you have a beef allergy. The 21-Day Purification Program isn't for pregnant or breastfeeding women — too much calorie restriction. If you're on warfarin or one of the newer blood thinners, anything green and leafy in the Standard Process catalog has enough vitamin K to mess with your INR — get your doctor's input. If you have kidney or liver problems, the MediHerb herbals are the ones to ask about. And in general: don't stack 4-8 different Standard Process products without a practitioner who's actually keeping track. The cumulative B-vitamins or iodine can add up.

What to Do If You Experience a Reaction

If a reaction occurs, the standard guidance is to stop the supplement and contact your healthcare provider. A clinician can review the full ingredient list, your other medications and supplements, and any underlying conditions that may be relevant. Drug and Supplement Interactions

Things to know if you're on prescriptions: blood thinners and the vitamin K in the green-vegetable Standard Process products — that's a real interaction. Thyroid medication and the glandulars or PMG — needs timing and TSH monitoring. Lithium and the diuretic MediHerb stuff — your doctor needs to track your serum lithium. Immunosuppressant drugs and Echinacea Premium — there's a theoretical concern. Statins and any of the cardiovascular Standard Process formulas — your doctor should run a lipid panel. The bigger practical issue is just stacking too many SKUs at once without supervision — the totals creep up.

Long-Term Use Considerations

Most people who do well on Standard Process just stay on the same products for months or years. Quick check-in windows: 8-12 weeks for how you feel, 12-16 weeks for any lab numbers to actually move, 6 months for whether you want to keep paying for it. Standard labs to track: a CBC, basic metabolic panel, lipid panel, TSH with free T4 (especially if you're on glandulars or PMG), vitamin D and B12 if you're on the relevant products. The 95-year operating history without a major recall is reassuring. The longer the practitioner's full Standard Process catalog review covers the long-term question.

Bottom line. If a real practitioner is putting you on Standard Process and paying attention, you'll probably do fine. The things to watch are: autoimmune thyroid disease and the PMG line, beef allergy and the glandulars, the 21-Day Purification Program when you're pregnant or breastfeeding (don't), and the vitamin K content if you're on warfarin. The brand really is well-made. The farm is real. The price is fair for some SKUs and not for others — which is exactly what a real clinician's review will tell you.
